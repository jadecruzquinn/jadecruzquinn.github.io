# Help content

**Source:** https://contentdesign.intuit.com/talking-to-customers/help/

---

Our help content is anything that helps explain what our users need to know about how to use our products. That might be a quick tip on a screen, an article on the web, or a chatbot conversation. When help is friendly, findable, and accurate, it makes people believe in our products and ultimately stick with us.

There are two general categories of help content: **quick in-line help** and **longer help articles**.

---

## In-line help content

### Planning in-line help content

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Use hyperlinks at strategic points throughout the product to give customers access to in-line help in the form of tooltips, guided tours, and/or PONs. | Link out to external (non-Intuit) articles, websites, or resources. |
| Make data-driven decisions whenever possible about where to place help content, what type of help to use, and which info to include. | Link out of the product to internal content (public relations articles, FAQs, support sites, etc.) unless it's truly the best way to help a customer move forward. |
| Make careful choices about when to keep or cut financial jargon. Sometimes we need to keep it and define it. Other times, it's better to cut it. | |
| Work with the right subject matter experts to make sure your content is correct from an accounting, tax, or technical perspective. | |

### 3 types of in-line help content

#### Tooltips
Tooltips are 2–3 line snippets that give customers quick, contextual info about a specific feature or piece of the experience. Use them to:
- Help customers complete a task
- Explain why we're collecting certain data
- Guide customers through a new experience
- Quickly highlight benefits or entitlements
- Point out a new feature

[Learn more on the Tooltips content patterns page](https://contentdesign.intuit.com/content-patterns/tooltips/)

#### Guided tours
A guided tour is a sequenced set of tooltips that guides customers step by step at the screen-level. Customers step through the tour by clicking next/back buttons or by taking a specific action. Use them to:
- Guide users step by step through a series of actions or tasks
- Call out or orient customers to a series of new product features or changes

#### Point-of-need (PONs)
PON links are longer, more in-depth answers to questions or concerns we expect customers to have as they interact with a specific screen or topic. Use them to:
- Provide lists of information
- Answer common questions about what to enter, where to enter it, why we're asking for certain info, or what to do if you don't have it
- Explain rules or guidelines
- Explain accounting, financial, or tax concepts and terminology

**Note:** PON content lives only within the software and is written to be very contextual to a specific screen or item. This is different from FAQs or help articles, which are written to be more general and can be accessed outside of the product.

**Writing PON links:**

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| What if none of these apply to me? | What if none of these apply to me. |
| If you're already enrolled | If you're already enrolled. |
| Why we're asking | Why we're asking. |
| Why don't I qualify for this credit? | Why don't I qualify for this credit. |
| Tell me more about payroll services | Tell me more about payroll services. |

Phrase help links in the voice of the customer as statements or questions. Occasionally, use "If" statements. Don't punctuate help links unless they're in the form of a question or exclamation.

Start each PON with a short, clear answer that includes the most basic info about the topic. To answer more specific or in-depth questions, add 1 or more expando links below the basic answer.

## Help articles

These are available both online and via the in-product Help menu. Types of help articles include:
- Concept and feature overviews
- Topical hubs
- Setup guides
- How-tos
- Advanced topics and troubleshooting
- FAQs
- References (link lists, legal, or compliance info)

---

## Goals for help

| Goal | What it means |
|------|---------------|
| **Empathy** | Get familiar with the customer problem. What does the customer need right now? Be human and offer a touch of reassurance if something might seem daunting. |
| **Simplicity** | Keep the customer moving forward. Don't overwhelm them with too much information. Don't overemphasize all the steps and complexities. Summarize and get going. |
| **Effectiveness** | Organize content so it's easy to scan and navigate. Make sure Google search is considered as well as PON contexts, and keep topics cross-linked if there are related tasks or info. |
| **Focus** | Answer the question the article is meant to solve. Organize content around customer needs and language, not internal needs. |

## Voice flexes for help

### Don't leave room for guesswork
Be as specific as possible with accounting concepts and tax law. However, when writing detailed steps, it's OK to say "Enter your info" instead of calling out every field. But it's not OK to generalize a process so much it leaves users guessing.

### Keep it positive
It's easy to tell folks what they can't do. Try to focus on providing assurance and resolution instead.

### Don't sell stuff
Help is about how to understand and use our products. It's fine to succinctly explain the benefit of a feature. But an article should never just be about why to use a feature, or come across like we're trying to sell something.

## Tips for creating help content

### Do your research
- Coordinate with help writers at least one month before any public product launch.
- Customers rarely know exactly what product they have. If you see a ton of related topics in search, it might be time to disambiguate the whole topic.
- What's out there already? If this is for one product, will info for other products show up in Google or in-product search?

### Answer the user's question
- Create action-oriented content. Be directive from the first word in the title.
- Create sections organized around specific actions.

### Keep it scannable
- Follow formatting guidelines.
- Customers don't want to know everything about a topic. Get to the point.
- Walls of text and long paragraphs make people give up.

### Don't forget content governance
If you only produce content and don't check how it's doing, it will get stale fast.

## Formatting

### Anatomy of a help article

Every article should have a title, summary, intro, and body (with organized headers).

#### Title

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Use 8 words max, ideally fewer for Google search relevance. | Punctuate unless it's a question. |
| Start with an action verb if you can. Example: Create and file 1099s with QuickBooks Online | Avoid gerunds (verbs ending in -ing). Example: Creating and filing 1099s with QuickBooks Online |
| | Use jargon. |

#### Summary
The summary shows just below the title. Ideally 1 sentence, 2 max. You can usually write it by finishing: "By reading this article, a customer should be able to learn _____."

Example: *Learn what to do if you can't reconcile your accounts.*

#### Intro

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Be brief. One short paragraph (2–4 sentences), maximum 2. Put other info under headers in the body. | Use lengthy "notes" or "before you begin" sections that tend to grow with random info over time. |
| Explain why your article matters and what to expect. | Try to position things negatively even if describing an issue. |

Example: *If you can't pay your entire tax bill right now, don't worry. Here's how you can request a payment plan from the IRS.*

#### Body
- Don't mix content types. Split how-to and troubleshooting info into separate articles.
- Use headers to organize everything into actionable or logical sections.

## Other help article elements

### Steps

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Keep numbered steps to 2–9 steps per section. | Include unnecessary steps. |
| Avoid nested steps. | |
| Be clear about where to start. | |
| Break into distinct sections under headers for long lists. | |
| Only use numbered steps for actions that must be sequential. | |
| Only bold the UI elements people actually select. | |
| Each step should be one action. | |

### Notes, callouts, warnings
- There's often no need to visually call out a note separately or in color.
- If you must add a note, bold the word Note and use a colon after.
- Highlighted callouts can be distracting. Use sparingly.

### Links

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Only link actions and be sure it's clear exactly where the link is taking the customer. | Say "Click here." |
| Link to other articles a customer will definitely need to complete a task or follow up on. | Create long lists of related content to link off to. |

### Bold and italics
- Only bold UI elements. Bolding too many things reduces scannability.
- Avoid italics in help content. The meaning or emphasis it conveys is not always clear.

### Images, animations, and video
Try to avoid images and animations. They're rarely necessary for comprehension, make it harder to quickly scan, get out of date quickly, and present accessibility issues.

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Use images, animations, or video only when not doing so would result in truly lengthy or awkward explanations. | Use images to show every screen your steps describe. |
| If there's an existing support video that covers the same subject, include it in the article intro. | |
