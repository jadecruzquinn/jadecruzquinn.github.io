# Our principles

**Source:** https://contentdesign.intuit.com/style-and-usage/our-principles/

---

## Talk to your friends

We know the basic grammar rules and some obscure ones, but we're not sticklers. When in doubt, we pick the conventions of everyday conversation over the grammar book.

Writing for Intuit is like having a chat with a friend. Our writing in Mailchimp, ProConnect, QuickBooks, and TurboTax is less formal and by-the-book than what you'll read in a newspaper—and that's OK.

**Example**
| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| You're all caught up. | All pending items have been processed. |

---

## Be clear and precise

Customers look to us for answers and guidance. So when you explain things, be as definitive and precise as you can. And use simple terms consistently.

Consistent use of definitive words improves user comprehension and also helps out with accessibility and translation.

There will be times when you don't know exactly what's going on. That's a good opportunity to push your partners to improve the experience so you can speak more directly. But if all else fails, try to be as straightforward as possible.

**Example**
| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| We can't connect to your Square account right now. Give us a few minutes. | Looks like there might be a problem with your Square account. Try again later. |

---

## End sentences with prepositions

It's fine to end sentences with prepositions most of the time. We prefer it, in fact. Just be careful not to slip into something too informal. If you'd never say it in conversation, then don't write it that way.

**Examples**
| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Share this story with anyone you talk finances with! | Share this story with anyone with whom you talk finances! |
| Where is it? | Where's it at? |
| Who should this invoice go to? | To whom should this invoice go? |
| What are you looking for? | For what are you looking? |

## Speak to customers as "you"

### Use first person when we talk about ourselves

Use *we*, *our*, and *us* when you write as TurboTax, QuickBooks, or ProConnect. It's one way that we make experiences feel more personal. We want customers to know that there are people behind Intuit products and that we're in this with them.

Just don't be creepy. We never want them to think we're spying on them.

**Exception:** When referring to the actual product, especially in marketing and sales, use *it*, not *us*.

**Examples**
| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| QuickBooks helps you get organized. It saves people about 8 hours per week. | QuickBooks helps you get organized. We save people about 8 hours per week. |
| We'll help you create professional-looking receipts and invoices. | We see your app hasn't updated since 3/10/10. Want us to update it now? |
| Still need help? Contact us. | |
| Pick a tax category and we'll take care of the rest. | |
| Let's get started. What's your email address? | Let's get started. Tell us your email address and we'll add it to your account. |

### Use second person for customers, except for buttons and legal stuff

Our product experiences are a conversation with customers. We talk directly to them, so use second person to address them.

Buttons can be the exception—sometimes they represent the customer's side of the conversation, so it's OK to use first person there to represent the customer's voice. Note: first person in buttons is an option, not a mandate. For more, [see guidelines on Actions](https://contentdesign.intuit.com/product-and-ui/actions/)

**Examples**
| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| I agree | You agree |
| Make any changes you want. | See a snapshot of where I stand. |
| See a snapshot of where you stand. | Enter an email address and phone number. |
| Enter your email address and phone number. | Connect your first account (button label) |
| Connect first account (button label) | Add all your customers (button label) |
| Add customers (button label) | 5 new customers were added this month! |
| You added 5 new customers this month! | |

### Use third person for people who aren't the customer

Try to keep things gender neutral, including pronouns. If you find yourself where the subject's gender is unknown, write your way around it. Don't use she/he, s/he, or one. If you can't write your way around it, then it's OK to use they, them, or their.

For more, check out our [gender-neutral language guidelines.](https://contentdesign.intuit.com/accessibility-and-inclusion/use-gender-neutral-language/)

## Think globally

### Translation differences

To make U.S. English copy work in other languages, things are going to change. Word order, grammatical gender, and copy length will affect the content design. In Romance languages like French, Portuguese, and Spanish, the content will be longer. Prepare to be flexible and work toward designs that can accommodate copy variations.

### Localization for culture fit

Direct translations of content aren't enough. Copy also needs to be culturally appropriate and correct for the region it appears in. Intuit products need to reflect local variations in currency, taxes, dates, times, government and business regulations, and other things that are important for our customers' finances.

### Writing tips that can help

#### Consistent, clear, specific word choice
Try to choose one specific term and stick to it. Instead of saying purchase, register, or get, maybe consistently write buy.

#### Contractions
Contractions are an essential way of making U.S. English conversational, but they get tricky in translation. Work with your regional content designers to decide how to handle them.

#### Gender
Try to be gender-neutral and inclusive in copy, and be aware that this is challenging in languages that build gender into the grammar.

#### Slang and jargon
Idioms are specific to cultures and languages. Use slang when it works, and partner with regional content partners to find the right fit for other regions.

#### Sentence length
Try to limit sentences to no more than 20 words.

#### Similes and metaphors
Some metaphors might not translate well. Feel free to use metaphors, but be careful and test it if you can.

**Examples**
| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Unlike real life, here you can easily visit your past. Just select the year you're interested in to step back in time. | Ready for a blast from the past? Step into our time machine and check out last year's data. |
| Think of interest rates and APR like a bill at a restaurant. The interest rate is just the cost of the food. But APR includes the food, tax, and tip. | Our servers are like insomniacs—every so often they need a medically-induced nap. |
| Everyone needs sleep, even our servers. They'll be down for maintenance 9:00–9:30 PM PT, so you can both get some shut-eye. | Think of an interest rate like the price of gas when you pay with cash, APR when you pay with a credit card. |

## Use active voice

We use active voice most of the time because it's clearer, more direct, and easier to read than passive voice. It's also more conversational.

- **Active voice:** The subject of the sentence performs the action.
- **Passive voice:** The subject receives the action and doesn't necessarily appear in the sentence.

| Active | Passive |
|-------------|-----------------|
| The team conducted usability tests with customers. | Usability tests were conducted with customers. |
| Quickly categorize your transactions. | Your transactions can quickly be categorized. |

Active voice also conveys responsibility. When we talk to customers, we're as transparent as possible.

**Examples**
| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| We're taking a look at the info you sent. | Your documents will be reviewed. |
| We couldn't save your changes. Try again in a bit. | Your changes weren't saved. |

### When passive voice is OK

Sometimes passive voice makes sense—for example, in confirmation messages or toast notifications where you're providing a quick status update:

| Passive confirmation (OK) | Active (too verbose) |
|---------------------------|----------------------|
| 34 invoices sent. | You sent 34 invoices. |
| Invoice 4107 sent to Travis Waldron | We sent invoice 4107 to Travis Waldron. |
| Changes saved | The system saved your changes. |

Passive voice in these contexts may sound less conversational than active voice. That’s OK if you’re being diagnostic or just providing a quick summary. You can also use passive voice when you want to soften the impact of a bad situation:

**Example**
| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Your account was suspended. Give us a call so we can help you get back on track. | We suspended your account. Contact us for more info. |

**In help and tooltips:** Use active voice as much as possible. In some cases, passive voice makes the info shorter and more concise. And it can be a good choice if the customer doesn’t need to know who performed the action.

Examples where passive is fine:
- E-file your 1099s early so they can be processed and sent to your contractors by January 31.
- First, make sure QuickBooks Desktop was properly installed on your server.
- Mapped drives aren't displayed and can't be monitored.

## Use everyday contractions

Use the contractions of everyday conversation. Just don't get carried away.

**Don't use:**
- Don't use regional contractions like ain't, shan't, y'all, mustn't
- Don't turn nouns into contractions

**Examples**
| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Thanks! We'll send you an email when we know more. | Your feedback's valuable to us. |
| It'll help later, we promise. | Thanks! An update's on the way. |
| Ready to create an invoice? | This'll help later, we promise. |
| We'll get you every deduction and credit you're entitled to. | Y'all fixin' to create an invoice? |
| The agent has spent over two hours with a customer. | The agent's spent over two hours with a customer. |

**Contractions that are usually fine:**

| | | |
|---|---|---|
| that's | you've | we're |
| shouldn't | you're | we'll |
| let's | you'll | wasn't |
| it's | won't | they've |
| isn't | where's | they're |
| haven't | what's | there's |
| hasn't | weren't | don't |
| doesn't | didn't | couldn't |
| can't | aren't | |

## Use simple verb tenses

For the most part, write in simple tenses—past, present, and future. They're direct, clear, and short. Try to stay away from progressive tenses unless you need to convey ongoing action.

Simple verb tenses are also easier for non-native speakers to understand and for translation teams to translate.

| Simple tense (do) | Complex tense (don't) |
|-------------------|----------------------|
| **Past:** You got a discount for QuickBooks | You had been getting a discount for months when you unsubscribed. / You were getting a discount when you unsubscribed. |
| **Present:** Review your transactions / You get a discount for QuickBooks | You've been getting a discount since January. / You're getting a discount. |
| **Future:** You'll get a discount for QuickBooks | You'll have been getting a discount for months when you unsubscribe. / You'll be getting a discount next year. |
