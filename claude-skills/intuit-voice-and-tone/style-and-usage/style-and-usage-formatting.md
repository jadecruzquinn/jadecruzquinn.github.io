# Formatting

**Source:** https://contentdesign.intuit.com/style-and-usage/formatting/

---

We keep additional formatting of copy at a minimum. The words themselves should be precise and presented in a clear enough way that they shouldn't require any additional emphasis or special treatment. In some cases, we do format copy for clarity. Follow these guidelines.

---

## Acronyms & abbreviations

When an acronym appears for the first time, spell it out and include the acronym in parentheses—unless it's commonly known, like IRS, ZIP code, EIN, PIN. For plurals, use a lowercase s without an apostrophe. Use standard abbreviations—don't just truncate words.

**Examples**
- Your Employer Identification Number (EIN) is a 9-digit number issued by the IRS. You can edit or change your EIN from the Company identification tab if you need to.
- SLAs, CPAs
- 111 Bell Ave., Apt. C
- Attn.

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Include the period of an abbreviation when using a colon (*Enter invoice no.:*) | Don't use the hash symbol (#) to mean number. (*Invoice #*) |
| In column headers, you can abbreviate "number" as "no." (*Invoice no.*) | Don't use acronyms for product names. Spell out QuickBooks Online, never QBO. (*Get your free QBO trial today!*) |
| For abbreviations in column headers, use a period at the end of the abbreviation. (*Ref. no.*) | Don't use Latin abbreviations. (*e.g., i.e.*) |

It's OK to shorten words like year, month, week, and quarter if you're short on space:

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| £9/wk, $29/mo, per yr, this qtr | Next mon, this mth |
| Use lowercase, no period | Don't capitalize or add periods |

## Bold

Don't use bold copy as a substitute for appropriately coded second- and third-level headings (like H2 and H3).

Use only one type treatment at a time (color, bold, indentation).

Use bold content sparingly—it can call attention to something important, but if overused, it makes a screen look chaotic, cluttered, and intimidating.

Use bold for:
- Terms that need emphasis (this is very rare)
- Transaction numbers or other business data, such as invoice numbers
- Names of user interface elements in help and support content 
- Navigation choices referred to in instructions
- User actions: buttons or steps mentioned in instructional content

**Examples**
| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Invoice **4107** is on its way to Travis Waldron. | **Invoice 4107** is on its way to Travis Waldron. |
| We'll cover that later in **Deductions and Credits**. | We'll cover that later in *Deductions and Credits*. |
| Select **Continue** to see your results. | Select **Continue** to see your **results**. |

## Capitalization

### Sentence case rules

Use sentence case, even in headings and titles. Sentence case is casual and friendly. It supports the conversational Intuit style and brand personality. It also makes translation a bit easier.

#### Don't capitalize feature names

Capitalizing features such as invoices, mileage tracking, and cash flow monitoring can be distracting and confusing for users. This form of sub-branding also dilutes the strength of the licensed, branded products we want to capitalize.

We capitalize the names of branded standalone products, add-on products that require a standalone product to use, and features that are licensed, branded, and unique to QuickBooks.

We don’t capitalize non-branded subfeatures or product-specific features.

Note that sometimes, the branded name of a QuickBooks product may also be used as a verb phrase in some instances (for example, QuickBooks Payroll vs. run payroll, QuickBooks Payments vs. accept payments). If you’re talking about the product, capitalize the name. If you’re talking about a feature or using the word as a generic description, don’t capitalize it.

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| payroll calculations | Payroll Calculations |
| mileage reports | Mileage Reports |
| QuickBooks Envelopes | quickbooks envelopes |
| QuickBooks Checking | quickbooks checking |
| QuickBooks Time | quickbooks time |

#### Use ALL CAPS only when you should

Avoid using ALL CAPITAL LETTERS. It's like screaming, and may present additional difficulty for users with dyslexia and other reading challenges or vision impairments. Use all caps only for badges (NEW, PLUS), some marketing pages, navigation labels, and table headers. All of these uses are exceptions. We really don’t need to scream at our customers that often.

**Exceptions for marketing**
To attract new customers and encourage action, marketers might capitalize the word free (Free or FREE). Research proves that the capitals are effective for conversion, but the promotional tone of the capital letters is out of place in the in-product conversation we’re having with our users.

#### The case for sentence case

- **Brand voice**: Sentence case supports the friendly, conversational tone we want. Also, if we mention a branded feature in a button, it stands out more if it’s capitalized, and such a presentation is better for that brand. Example: Start my ExpenseFinder trial
- **Readability**: Easier to read and comprehend, especially for CTAs more than three words. Breadcrumb labels in sentence case are easier to scan.
- **Font size**: Capital letters might occupy more space, limiting our ability to simplify the interface.
- **Localization**: Sentence case is generally easier to translate for non-U.S. markets.
- **Consistency**: Easier to teach and get consistent copy. Training in sentence case helps prevent mistakes like "Start your Free Trial."

### Terms we capitalize

| Type | Examples |
|------|---------|
| Proper nouns | English, James Bond |
| Proper names | QuickBooks Online Self-Employed, Franchise Tax Board, Internal Revenue Service, West Virginia |
| Popular names | the Badlands (in South Dakota), "the Series" for the World Series |
| Derivatives | Shakespearean, Californian |
| Titles before names | President Lincoln received a cordial reception (lowercase otherwise: The president received a cordial reception.) |

#### Product navigation
This is an ongoing discussion we're having across Intuit. While we would dearly love for product navigation to be sentence case, there are challenges around making the change consistently throughout the products. Match the case to the rest of the nav if it would look awkward otherwise.

### Some tax terms appear in title case

Use title case for:
- Deduction or credit names: *Let's see if you qualify for the Child Tax Credit.*
- Sections: *Later in Personal Info, we'll gather more details.*

Use lowercase for:
- Federal and state: *How to file your state taxes online*
- Filing status: *The head of household status can lead to a lower taxable income.*

## Italics

Try to avoid italics in copy. If you need to guide the user to a UI element, use bold instead.

Only use one treatment (color, bold, indentation) to emphasize text. For example, if the placeholder text in a form field is a distinct color, italics are unnecessary.

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| To get started, select **Apps** in the menu. | To get started, select *Apps* in the menu. |
| Select **Continue** to see your results. | Select *Continue* to see your results. |

## Links

Links guide users to other destinations or files.

### Guidelines

- Avoid in-line links for accessibility. If you must use them, make sure they're underlined.
- Don't put multiple links to the same place on the same page.
- Don't add links to every possible resource—present the most helpful ones.
- Don't use links in headlines.
- Punctuate based on context. For CTAs in the product, create a separate linked CTA for accessibility rather than linking within a sentence. Example: "Have a question? Email us" vs "If you have a question, email us."
- For legal details, link in the footer with language like "For more details, check out the terms of use."

## Lists

### Bulleted lists

Bulleted lists help organize content and make it easier to scan. Keep them short (no more than 5 items) and avoid more than one bulleted list on a single screen.

#### Rules for bullets

- If your bullets complete a lead-in sentence, or are single words/fragments, don't start them with a capital letter or end them with punctuation.
- Be consistent with structure. Make all bullets either fragments or complete sentences, starting the same way (all verbs or all nouns).
- Introduce the list with a lead-in sentence, fragment, or heading. Use a question mark if it's a question.

### Numbered lists

Use numbered lists to show the order in which actions should occur, when events will take place, or order of importance.

1. Use a period at the end of each list item if it's a complete sentence, and keep punctuation consistent. Don't use commas or semicolons.
2. Write all items in the same voice (usually active) and tense (usually present).
3. Fragments are fine, but if one item is a complete sentence, try to write them all that way. Or turn that odd sentence into a fragment.
4. Make sure each step includes only one action or two closely related actions. Example: "Go to the menu and select Apps." That helps keep the steps simple.
5. Use parallel construction for your list items. If one item starts with a verb, every item should start with a verb.
6. Don't use "and," "or," or "and/or" at the beginning of any list item.
7. Begin each numbered item with an initial capital letter unless you’re using the list items to complete the sentence.
8. Introduce the list with a lead-in sentence, fragment, heading, or question.

## Mathematical symbols and equations

Mathematical symbols (+, -, x, /, ÷, =) and equations are permissible but should not substitute words. For accessibility, explain the equation first—not all screen readers will read these symbols. Explaining the equation helps all users understand it.

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Add your net income after taxes and your non-cash expenses. Then divide that number by your liabilities... | (Net income after tax + non-cash expenses) ÷ all liabilities = solvency ratio (without explanation first) |
| QuickBooks + Square (for a partnership) | Voice + tone, hold + drag (don't use + to mean "and" or give instruction) |
| Spell out: add, subtract, multiply, divide | 2.5x or 10x (don't use x to mean "times") |

## Tables

Use tables primarily for numerical information. Don't include full sentences—or worse, paragraphs—in tables.

### Table guidelines

- Formatting should be consistent within the table and with any nearby tables.
- Don't use tables to condense lists or include text beyond a few words per cell.
- If possible, fit the size of the table to the viewer displaying it.
- Avoid tables within tables for online content.
- Set off table headings and row headings if needed. Usually, a table title is unnecessary.
- Use the same font in the table as the surrounding content. Don't use special fonts or font sizes.

### Cell alignment

| Cell type | Alignment |
|-----------|-----------|
| Column headings – text | Left-aligned |
| Column headings – numbers | Center-aligned |
| Short words (3 letters or less) | May look better centered |
| Words in columns | Left-aligned |
| Numbers in columns | Right-aligned, decimal-aligned, or centered |

> **Note**: Tables present major accessibility issues. Screen readers have a hard time reading through table content. Use tables thoughtfully.

---

## Text alignment

| Alignment | When to use | Rationale |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| Left | Default for paragraphs and text longer than 2 lines.  | Since our eyes jump fast down left-aligned content, it's easier and quicker to read. Left-aligned text makes things look more polished and aesthetically pleasing. |
| Center | Headlines and short, scannable lines (1–2 lines max) | When reading center-aligned content, our eyes have to scan both sides of the text. This takes us longer than reading left-aligned text since we have to find where the next line begins. |
| Right | Numbers and times in tables, cards, and widgets | Numbers are easy to compare at a glance with the left-aligned info opposite it. |
| Justified | Never use | Irregular spacing is hard to read. |

## Underlines

Don't underline copy unless it's for links.

- **Inline links** should be underlined (or other non-color visual distinction) for accessibility.
- **Links that stand alone** do not need to be underlined because they have ample white space to be recognizable as interactive.

## URLs

When we spell out a URL, use lowercase, even if it refers to a product name. Leave out the scheme/protocol (http, https) and the www part.

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Need more info? Check out developer.intuit.com | https://www.developer.intuit.com |
| Go to quickbooksconnect.com | https://QuickBooksConnect.com |
| Start now at turbotax.intuit.com | http://www.TurboTax.intuit.com |
