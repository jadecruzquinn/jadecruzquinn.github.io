# Punctuation

**Source:** https://contentdesign.intuit.com/style-and-usage/punctuation/

---

We outline specific punctuation guidelines for commas, dashes, exclamation points, and more. If something's not covered here, check the Associated Press Stylebook.

Other countries and languages have their own guidelines for punctuation. In translated and localized versions of our products, work with regional content designers to make sure the punctuation is correct and helpful.

---

## Ampersands

Don't use ampersands in body text or most headers, unless they're part of a company name, title, or other proper noun.

**Exception**: To save space, you can use ampersands in navigation, tweets, and chart and table headers.

The subjects joined by the ampersand must be related.

Note: Ampersands might create issues for translation and localization.

Don't use the plus symbol (+) to mean "and" unless referring to a partnership, like QuickBooks + Square.

---

## Apostrophes

Use apostrophes to form contractions, possessives, and (in rare cases) plurals.

For joint possession, you only need a single apostrophe. Including apostrophes for each possessor means they each have their own individual thing.

### Examples

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Jack's and Jill's teams support two completely different business functions. | Jack and Jill's teams (implies joint possession) |
| Jack and Jill's concept pitch moved the audience to tears. | Jack's and Jill's concept pitch (implies they had separate pitches) |
| The CPA's opinion is that many CPAs love TurboTax. | — |
| Yes's and no's | Yes' and no's |
| Do's and don'ts | Dos and don'ts |

### Contractions

Write like we talk—use the contractions of everyday conversation.

| Do | Don't (regional contractions)|
|----|-------|
| It's | Y'all |
| You'll | Mustn't |
| They're | Shan't |
| Let's | Ain't |

### Awkward apostrophes

If it looks or sounds awkward, rewrite to avoid the apostrophe:

| Instead of | Use |
|------------|-----|
| our customers' feelings | how our customers feel |
| Phyllis's first foray into payments | the first time Phyllis took a job in payments |
| your business's growth | the growth of your business |

Don't turn nouns into contractions (like "Your appointment's been cancelled"). These phrases are tricky to translate.

## Asterisks

An asterisk flags a corresponding comment at the bottom of the page. When possible, avoid them—they slow web readers down. You’re interrupting their train of thought and asking them to hunt for secondary information.

### Legal guidelines

| Use | When |
|-----|------|
| One asterisk (*) | Promo or offer |
| Two asterisks (**) | Features or product information |

The asterisk goes before a dash, but after every other punctuation mark.
Don't put the asterisk in the middle of a sentence. Put it at the end.
Consult with your legal contact to identify how your legal content should be formatted.

**Examples**
| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Save up to 50%—sale ends Friday.* | Now with 50% more beef* and voted #1 in the world!** *(multiple in one sentence)* |
| Health benefits for your team, provided by [partner]** | Health benefits* for your team, provided by [partner]* *(asterisk in the middle of a sentence)* |
| With a built-in 1.00% APY bank account, you can easily access and grow your money.** | Now with 50% more beef* and more delicious! *(no legal use defined)* |

Don't use multiple asterisks in one sentence—this causes accessibility issues (screen readers) and is visually jarring.

## Brackets

We rarely use angle brackets < >. Don't use angle brackets > to separate UI elements. Nobody talks that way. Instead, write out the path with the word "select."

**Example**
| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Go to Taxes, then select Payroll Tax. | Select Taxes > Payroll Tax |

Use square brackets [ ] to insert text inside quotes for: clarifying unstable pronouns, translation, indicating a change in capitalization, censoring objectionable content, or a parenthetical within a parenthetical.

**Example**: We're huge fans of this [point of sale] system because it integrates with QuickBooks.

---

## Colons

Use a colon to introduce a list when the introductory text is a complete sentence.

When using an abbreviation with a colon, include the period (e.g., "Invoice no.:").

If the phrase after a colon is an independent clause, capitalize the first letter after the colon (or better yet, use a period).

If the phrase after a colon is a dependent clause, don't capitalize the first letter after the colon.

Don't use colons in headlines and subheads—if you feel like you need one, see if there's space for a subheading instead.

**Examples**
| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Add the sales tax components: component names, agencies, and percentages. | Add the sales tax components, including component names, agencies, and percentages: | 
| We've got something new for you: accounting, payments, and payroll | (colon in a heading) |

## Commas

Use the **serial (Oxford) comma**: Include a comma before the conjunction in a series of three or more.

**Examples**
| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Rely on TurboTax to get your deductions, credits, and more. | Rely on TurboTax to get your deductions, credits and more. |
| Track your income, pay bills, or run payroll. | Track your income, pay bills or run payroll. |

### Commas in dates

Use a comma after the year when starting a phrase or clause with a date. Use commas when writing month, day, and year.
Don’t use commas for only the month and year or the month and day.

**Examples**
| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Your taxes are due April 15, 2020. | Your taxes are due April 15 2020. |
| Starting April 30, 2021, we are increasing the price of premium services. | January, 2021 was a busy month. |
| On April 15, 2015, he was living in Los Angeles. | The year starts on January, 1. |

### Commas with conjunctions

Use a comma between two clauses that can stand alone and are linked by a conjunction.
You can use “then” as a coordinating conjunction (even though technically it’s not) when it makes the sentence a quicker read.

Note: Compound sentences tend to be longer and add a layer of complexity. Don’t overdo them.

**Examples**
| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Go to the store, then go to the bank. | Go to the store then go to the bank. |
| You can create purchase orders and adjust inventory items. | You can create purchase orders, and adjust inventory items. |

Don't use a comma with a single subject and two verbs. It creates an awkward pause and just looks weird.

### Commas with salutations

Use a comma after a salutation or direct address ("Hello, Tom"). For simple "Hi," you can omit the comma ("Hi Tom").

### Commas in currency

Use a comma in monetary amounts if it's standard to do so (e.g., $5,000,000 in the US).

## Dashes and hyphens

Dashes and hyphens can connect ideas, create emphasis, or add helpful context—but they're not interchangeable.

| Mark | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| Hyphen (-) | Connects compound words |
| En dash (–) | Shows range or relationship |
| Em dash (—) | Interrupts, adds emphasis |

### Hyphens

A hyphen makes compound words. Use compound adjectives sparingly.

**Rules**:
- Use a hyphen if using a number with a unit of measure as an adjective (100-yard dash).
- Don't include a space on either side of a hyphen or dash.
- Don't use a hyphen for "sign in" unless it's an adjective (sign-in screen).
- Never hyphenate verbs like "set up" when the noun version is a single word (setup).

**Examples**
| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| You can review your taxes on a 12-week schedule. | Sign-in to your account |
| Get one-on-one help from a tax expert. | Set-up your account (verb form) |
| Run in-depth sales reports. | non-taxable (common prefixes don't need hyphens) |

#### For common prefixes, don't hyphenate

| Prefix | Without hyphen | Exception |
|--------|---------------|-----------|
| non- | nontaxable, nonprofit, nonresident, noninventory | non-sufficient, non-posting |
| anti- | antivirus, antitrust | — |
| bi- | biennial, biannual | — |
| co- | coworker, copay | — |
| multi- | multicolor, multilevel, multicultural | multi-user |
| e- | — | e-commerce, e-file, e-pay |
| sub- | — | sub-category |
| pre- | preassigned, predefined, preexisting, preselected | |

### En dashes

An en dash (–) expresses a range of values (times, years, dollar amounts).

| Example | Use |
|---------|-----|
| Aug 27–Sep 1 | Date range spanning months |
| Sep 18–24 | Date range within a month |
| 2004–2016 | Year range |
| 7–9 PM | Time range |
| $500–$800 | Price range |

### Em dashes

An em dash (—) creates a pause or interruption—like a semicolon, but with more personality. Use them intentionally.

| Use | Example |
|-----|---------|
| Conversational pause | Something didn't go as planned—let's try that again. |
| Adding helpful context | You can update your info at any time—no approval needed. |
| Landing a point | There's just one thing left—review and submit. |
| Tone over parentheses | You'll need your 1099—your client can provide it if you don't have it handy. |

| Don't use | Instead |
|-----------|---------|
| Don't use for connecting two complete sentences | Use a period: "Your return is pending. We've filed it." |
| Don't use for interrupting a sentence more than once | Use a comma: "If you qualify based on income, you may receive a credit." |
| Don't use spaces around the em dash | No spaces: "cats and dogs—they're cute" |

## Ellipses

Try to avoid. Ellipses can make you seem less than confident.

Use an ellipsis (…) to indicate:
- An omission in quoted text
- Action in the background (loading)
- Overflow text
- Hesitation, faltering speech, or thoughts

Make sure there's no space between the last letter and the first period.
Don’t use ellipses for dramatic effect or in place of commas or dashes.

**Examples**
| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Almost there… | Profits you can picture… so you can plan |
| Checking for updates… | Click Customers > Enter Vehicle Mileage… |
| "It's highly professional yet so easy to use… I'd be lost without it." | — |

For sensitive information, use midline dots (●) instead of ellipses.

## Exclamation points

Express enthusiasm with exclamations—just make sure they sound like a natural reaction.

Exclamations work best with single words and short phrases. Use sparingly.

**Rules**:
- Don't follow with another punctuation mark (unless part of a title or trademark).
- Never use double (!!) or triple (!!!) exclamation points.
- Don't use two or three exclamatory words in a row.
- Use visual supplements (color, illustrations, motion) if you need to amplify excitement.

**Examples**
| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Way to go! You created your first profit and loss report. | Sign up for QuickBooks and get your first month free! |
| Nice! You completed your first payroll run. | Get the first 30 days free!!! |
| Congratulations! You filed your taxes. Here's what's next. | Do you really want to delete these transactions? Yikes! |
| Great! Let's start by getting to know you. | Uh-oh! We couldn't sync your app with QuickBooks. |
| Yahoo!, Google, and Apple | Can you believe it?! |
| Quarterly and year-end tax relief is here! | New! Track your tax refund!! |

## Hashtags

Use camel case for multiword hashtags (#LikeThis) for readability and accessibility. Screen readers can discern camel case hashtags as separate words. All-lowercase can be read as gobbledygook.

**Examples**
| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Attn. #SmallBiz: Here's how to use #PokemonGo to lure more customers | Invoice # *(don't use # for the word "number")* |
| Invoice no. | #smallbiz (all lowercase hashtag) |

## Parentheses

Use parentheses in pairs to provide examples, add an aside, or introduce an abbreviation.

Your sentence should make sense when read out loud without the parenthetical text.

- If a sentence ends with a parenthetical that's only part of a larger sentence, the period goes **outside** the closing parenthesis.
- If the parenthetical itself is a whole sentence, the period goes **inside** the parenthesis.

### Examples
- A balance sheet report calculates how much your business is worth (your business's equity) by subtracting all the money your company owes (liabilities) from everything it owns (assets).
- Protect your account by turning on multifactor authentication (MFA).
- Federal exempt forms (like IRS 501c3 forms) don't apply to state sales tax.

## Percent symbol

Use the percent symbol (%) with numerals. Use the word "percent" in other instances.

**Examples**
| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| 85% of attendees agreed to be interviewed. | 85 percent of attendees agreed. |
| We don't know the percent of returns like yours. | We don't know the % of returns like yours. |
| Your taxes are down 12%. | Your taxes are down twelve percent. |

---

## Periods

Use periods to end a sentence, unless it ends with a question mark or exclamation point. Use a period at the end of an abbreviation. If a sentence ends with an abbreviation, you don’t need an additional period.

Don't use periods in headlines or email subjects. Preheaders are OK.

Place one space after periods, not two.

**A note about headlines**: Headlines should be crisp and digestible. Adding periods to headlines may slow readers as they move down the page. You can use periods at the end of a sentence or fragment if you’re going for impact.

| Type | Do | Don't |
|------|-----|-------|
| Headlines | Snap a photo of your receipt | Snap a photo of your receipt. |
| Headlines with exclamation | Congratulations! You're approved. | Congratulations! You're approved! (two exclamations) |
| Sentences | QuickBooks knows how to write a full sentence. | QuickBooks knows how to write a full sentence |

## Pipes

Use a pipe | (vertical bar) in title tags to separate elements.

### Examples

- Pipes | Punctuation | Intuit content design
- Primary keyword | Secondary keyword | Brand name

---

## Question marks

Use a question mark at the end of a direct question.

Don't use more than one together (???) or pair with an exclamation point (?!).

### Examples

- Did we get this right?
- How do you want to enter your income?

---

## Quotation marks

Use quotation marks for spoken words or short quoted phrases.

- Put commas and periods **inside** closing quotation marks.
- Other punctuation (colons, semicolons, question marks, exclamation points) goes **outside** closing quotation marks, unless part of the quote.
- Avoid using quotation marks for emphasis—bold words or phrases instead.

### Examples
- I'm not sure we need "each month"—should we just end with "saves us money"?
- "It's a huge efficiency," he said, "that also saves us money each month."
- "Just sorted four months of transactions in 20 minutes."

---

## Semicolons

Don't use semicolons. They're too formal for digital content. Instead, separate independent clauses with a period or with conjunctions like "and," "or," "but," and "so."

**Example**
| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Let's say you're creating an invoice. Go to Create (+) invoice. | Let's say you're creating an invoice; go to Create (+) invoice. |

---

## Slashes

Use slashes when necessary to mean "per," "and," or "or."

Don't use spaces on either side of the slash.

### Examples

- 2020/2021 fiscal year
- c/o (care of), P/E (price-to-earnings ratio), w/ (with)
- Enter an item in the Product/Service field.
- Contact us for 24/7 support.
- $10/month
